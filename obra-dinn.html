Good of course in that it is a unique experience, but also bad in that nothing else concieved by us can capture what this game did again.

My wife and I solved this game together, we looked nothing up and still got all the fates and the "true ending." (We did admittedly trial and error JUST a little.)

Just after the true ending is where this entry takes place, I pictured the investigator sitting down at their desk and finally getting out some thoughts about all this.

Below are the paragraphs that came out of me channeling that. Enjoy.

TRANSCRIPT

Thus with this last vision the awful ordeal of the Obra Dinn is closed.
That "witty" title is what the major publications took to calling it shortly after my investigation.
They claimed to have the story, they did not.
A terrible storm, an outbreak of ocean creatures known to us, "rumors" of a curse that lead to betrayal and mutine. Barely a quarter of the truth, but, they could never know the full truth, how could they?
How could I without seeing it with my own eyes?
When I set foot on that boat I believed that on old sea maps when there were unknown waters that ships rarely returned from they wrote "Here there be monsters." I did not believe that they were correct. I did not believe believe until I saw the creatures, the death they left in their wake because of the greed and treachery of a few souls. "Terrible Beast" is all I could bring myself to pen them down as in my report. As if the actions of those sea folk could compare to the Second Mates heinous acts. He was the one who pitted the Obra Dinn into a war none of the crew signed up for. Can one blame the soldiers for the carnage of war? Though, here with my thoughts I can wax on about the injustice and the wrongdoing; but in reality I am no expert in that field. My report did not result in any justice doled out, or a widespread knowledge of the tales of heorism carried out by the decent men aboard that ship. My investigations end in lines on a ledger - dollar amounts next to destruction, the finances of fate. I of course strive to do my job well, to analyze the facts fairly and ensure my reports are accurate and the money rightfully owed notated clearly. Perhaps thats why Henry Evans trusted me with the watch.
The watch. It haunts me to this day. And "haunts" is truly the best word for it. How else would you describe seeing apperitions of the dead after all?
It's more than that though, I know this deep down. Its abilities vex and bewitch me. When I lay down to rest I cannot help but wonder if the watchs power stems from hearing the final cries of a soul being snuffed out and in desperation to hear what it has to say it manafests its haunting; or, is the watch merely drawn to the act of death?
I hope it is the former. I fear it is the latter.
The grim visage of death adorning the front of its case lends more credence to the second theory. Not to mention the very nature of its power is itself a "devils bargain." I get to know, but, in exchange someone must die. Evans knew this. It's why he shot that monkey so that I could know what went on in the lazarette. It was only an animal but still, an exhange was made. Not one I made, even though exchanges are my speciality, but I was still the one who got to know.
Is it a deal I would make if the situation calls?
I cannot say for sure.
For now, I am the one who gets to know. The price of that knowlege always hovering over my head, and always notated in my ledger.
I think it is time for a holiday.
I've locked the watch away for now, until next I am to be haunted.
I need some tea, or perhaps something stronger.
